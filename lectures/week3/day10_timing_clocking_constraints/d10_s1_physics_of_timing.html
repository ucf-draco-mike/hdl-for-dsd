Why Timing Matters

Every gate and wire has propagation delay. Signals take time to travel through combinational logic between flip-flops.

Timing determines the maximum clock frequency of your design.
Every gate and wire has delay. Timing determines your max clock frequency. If signals arrive late, flip-flops capture garbage.

Setup & Hold Time

               Tsetup      Thold
              ◄──────►    ◄──────►
              │        │          │
   data: ─────────────STABLE───────────────
              │        │          │
   clk:  ────────────┐ │
                      └───────────────
                      ↑
                  clock edge

Setup violation: data changes too close before the edge → wrong value captured

Hold violation: data changes too soon after the edge → wrong value captured

Fix: reduce combinational delay, pipeline, or slow the clock

Setup time: data stable before the edge. Hold time: data stable after. Violate either and the flip-flop captures garbage.

Critical Path & Slack

Critical path = longest delay between any two flip-flops in the design

Slack = clock_period − (Tclk-to-q + Tcomb + Trouting + Tsetup)

Positive slack → timing met ✓

Negative slack → timing violated ✗ (intermittent failures!)

At 25 MHz (40 ns period), most simple iCE40 designs have plenty of margin. Timing becomes critical at higher frequencies or with deep combinational chains.
Critical path is the longest delay. Slack is your margin. Positive = good. Negative = intermittent failures. At 25 MHz with 40 ns, most simple designs are fine.

Key Takeaways

① Every gate/wire has delay. Signals must arrive before setup time.

② Critical path = longest delay = limits max frequency.

③ Slack = clock_period − total_delay. Positive = pass. Negative = fail.

④ 25 MHz (40 ns) gives plenty of margin for simple iCE40 designs.
