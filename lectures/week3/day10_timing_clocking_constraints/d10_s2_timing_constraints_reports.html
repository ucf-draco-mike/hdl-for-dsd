Adding Timing Constraints

# Tell nextpnr about your clock
nextpnr-ice40 --hx1k --package vq100 \
    --pcf go_board.pcf \
    --json top.json --asc top.asc \
    --freq 25
--freq 25 tells nextpnr: “My clock is 25 MHz. Ensure all paths meet timing.” Without it, no timing check happens.
The --freq flag tells nextpnr your target frequency. Without it, no timing analysis. Always specify it.

Reading the Timing Report

# PASS — Fmax exceeds target
Info: Max frequency for clock 'i_clk': 48.23 MHz
#  Target: 25 MHz → PASS (23 MHz of margin)

# FAIL — Fmax below target
Info: Max frequency for clock 'i_clk': 22.10 MHz
#  Target: 25 MHz → FAIL! Timing violated.
#  Slack: -2.9 ns
Timing violations cause intermittent failures — the design works sometimes, fails randomly. The worst kind of bug.
Check max frequency against your target. Above = pass. Below = timing violation. These cause intermittent failures — the worst bugs.

Key Takeaways

① --freq 25 enables timing analysis. Always include it.

② Max frequency > target → pass. Below → violation.

③ Options when timing fails: pipeline, restructure logic, slow clock.

④ Timing violations cause intermittent bugs — never ignore them.
