SPI: Synchronous Serial

4 wires: SCLK (clock), MOSI (master out), MISO (master in), CS_N (chip select)

Master provides clock — no baud rate negotiation needed.

Data clocked in and out simultaneously — full duplex.

SPI is essentially two shift registers connected by a clock wire. You already have all the building blocks.
SPI: 4 wires, master provides clock. Full duplex — data flows both directions simultaneously. It's really just two connected shift registers.

SPI Transaction

  CS_N:  ────┐                               ┌──────
             └───────────────────────────────┘

  SCLK:  ────┐  ┌──┐  ┌──┐  ┌──┐  ┌──┐  ┌──┐  ┌──┐  ┌──
             └──┘  └──┘  └──┘  └──┘  └──┘  └──┘  └──┘  └──

  MOSI:  ────|D7|D6|D5|D4|D3|D2|D1|D0|──── (master → slave)

  MISO:  ────|D7|D6|D5|D4|D3|D2|D1|D0|──── (slave → master)

Assert CS_N low → clock 8 bits → deassert CS_N

After 8 clocks, master and slave have exchanged bytes.

SPI transaction: assert chip select, clock 8 bits in both directions simultaneously, deassert. After 8 clocks, bytes are exchanged.

UART vs. SPI vs. I²C

UARTSPII²C
Wires2 (TX, RX)4 (SCLK, MOSI, MISO, CS)2 (SDA, SCL)
ClockAsync (agreed baud)Sync (master provides)Sync (master provides)
DuplexFullFullHalf
DevicesPoint-to-point1 master, N slaves (N CS lines)Multi-master, N slaves (addr)
Speed~1 Mbps10+ Mbps100/400 Kbps
UseDebug, GPS, BTFlash, ADC, displaySensors, EEPROM
UART: simple point-to-point. SPI: fast, full duplex, multiple slaves. I2C: fewest wires, addressed multi-device, slower. Each has its niche.

Key Takeaways

① SPI: 4 wires. Master provides clock — no baud negotiation.

② Full duplex: data flows both ways simultaneously.

③ Four modes (CPOL/CPHA) — check the device datasheet.

④ SPI = connected shift registers. Same building blocks as UART.
