Where to Go From Here

Senior design projects (UCF): Robotics control, accelerator ASICs, embedded vision — any project with a hardware block. Your module library covers the I/O; add domain logic on top.

Graduate study: Computer architecture (EEL 5707), VLSI design (EEL 5771), hardware security (EEL 5779), advanced FPGA (CDA 5937). You're prepared for any of these.

Industry internships: FPGA engineer at defense primes (L3Harris, Northrop, Raytheon in Orlando); chip companies (Intel, AMD, Nvidia, Apple); verification engineer is a broad entry path.

Open-source silicon: Google Skywater MPW shuttles (free tape-outs!), OpenROAD, Tiny Tapeout program — you can now design a real ASIC for zero dollars.

Hobbyist path: Build a game console. Recreate a retro CPU (6502, Z80). Build a music synthesizer. Your RTL skills + Go Board + YouTube = endless weekends.

Research: UCF's DRACO Lab (hardware security), ECE departments at top research universities. Your assertion + coverage skill maps directly to DV research.

Six concrete paths. UCF-specific where possible. Plenty of options, each enabled by the course.

A Few Honest Notes

This course was accelerated. A normal Digital System Design course is 14 weeks; you did 16 days. That means some topics got brief treatment — arbitration, CDC fundamentals, memory hierarchies, buses and arbitration, interconnect standards (AXI, AHB, Wishbone). If any of these appear in senior design or a job interview, you'll need to supplement.

You will forget things. The Verilog syntax, the synthesis report format, the SVA operators — these will fade without use. That's fine. What you won't forget is the shape of the problem: what a testbench should do, what a module should look like, what a good commit looks like. That's the durable part.

The field is moving. AI-assisted design, open-source silicon, HLS, formal verification — the ground is shifting under the discipline. Your foundation is solid, but stay curious. What you learned in 2026 is the starting point, not the endpoint.

You belong. Hardware is an old, sometimes gatekept discipline. You showed up, did the work, built the thing. That makes you part of it. Don't let anyone tell you otherwise.

Honesty time. What wasn't covered, what will fade, what's shifting, what's earned. Students need to hear this. “You belong” is the whole point of ending with a retrospective.
