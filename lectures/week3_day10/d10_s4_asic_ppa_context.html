FPGA vs. ASIC PPA

FPGA (what we do)

Area: LUT count (fixed-size units)

Perf: Fmax from place-and-route

Power: Estimate from toggle rate

Tool: yosys stat + nextpnr

ASIC (industry)

Area: Gate count × cell size (nm²)

Perf: Critical path through std cells

Power: Dynamic + leakage (process node)

Tool: OpenROAD / OpenLane

Key difference: On FPGA, all LUTs are the same size. On ASIC, a 2-input gate is physically smaller than a 4-input gate. Area optimization is much richer.
On FPGA, area is quantized — LUTs are fixed. On ASIC, a simple inverter is tiny, a multiplier is large, and you pay for every transistor. The analysis habits are identical, but the numbers mean different things.

The insight: What's "expensive" depends on the platform. A 32-bit multiplier that consumes 30% of the iCE40 is trivial on an ASIC (or on an FPGA with DSP blocks).

Context matters. A 32-bit multiplier is massive on iCE40 but trivial on an ASIC or a larger FPGA with DSP. The ability to quantify cost and make informed decisions — that's what PPA analysis gives you.

Pre-Class Self-Check

Q1: What does positive slack mean? What about negative slack?

Positive slack = timing met (signal arrives before the deadline). Negative slack = timing violation (intermittent failures).

Q2: Why does assign product = a * b; use so many LUTs on iCE40?

iCE40 has no DSP blocks. Multiplication is implemented entirely in LUT logic, with O(N²) gate count growth. Larger FPGAs use dedicated DSP slices.

Pre-Class Self-Check (cont.)

Q3: When would you choose a sequential (shift-and-add) multiplier over a combinational one?

When area is constrained and latency is acceptable. Sequential uses O(N) LUTs but takes N cycles. Combinational uses O(N²) LUTs but finishes in 1 cycle.

Q4: What are the three FPGA PPA proxies, and which tools measure them?

Performance → Fmax (nextpnr --freq). Area → LUT/FF/EBR count (yosys stat). Power → toggle rate × capacitance (limited on iCE40, conceptual).
