The same Verilog source file gets consumed by two very different tools. Synthesis, Yosys in our case, converts it to real hardware primitives. Simulation, Icarus Verilog, models behavior computationally and produces waveforms.

What Synthesis Ignores

These constructs are simulation-only:

initial begin              // ‚Üê Can't "start up" hardware
    #10;                   // ‚Üê Can't tell gates to "wait"
    $display("x = %b", x); // ‚Üê No printf in silicon
    $dumpfile("dump.vcd");  // ‚Üê Waveform dump is a sim concept
    $finish;               // ‚Üê Hardware doesn't "finish"
end
Key insight: Testbenches are simulation artifacts. Design modules must avoid these constructs.
This is critical. initial blocks, delay values, display statements, dumpfile, finish ‚Äî all simulation-only. Synthesis ignores them completely. Your testbenches are full of these. Your synthesizable design must avoid them.

The Hidden Danger: Sim/Synth Mismatch

// In a DESIGN module (not testbench) ‚Äî DON'T DO THIS
always @(a or b) begin
    y = a & b;
    #10;           // Synthesis IGNORES this
    z = a | b;
end
Simulation: y updates, waits 10 units, then z updates.
Synthesis: #10 ignored. Both update simultaneously.
The simulation lies to you. It shows sequential behavior that won't exist in hardware.
Here's the real danger. If you put a delay in a design module, simulation models the delay. But synthesis ignores it. Your simulation says one thing, your hardware does another. This is a simulation-synthesis mismatch.

The Workflow Golden Rule

Simulate first. Synthesize second. Program last.

‚úó You cannot set breakpoints on an FPGA.

‚úó You cannot print from inside the chip (until Week 3 ‚Äî UART)

‚úì Waveform simulation is your primary debugging tool.

This is the golden rule of this course. Simulate first, synthesize second, program last. You cannot set breakpoints on an FPGA. You cannot print from inside the chip until we build UART in Week 3. Waveform simulation is your primary debugging tool.
